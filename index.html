SCIENCE, CYBORGS AND WOMEN

If we talk about the intersection between technology and political philosophy at the end of the 20th century, Haraway is the place to stop. With it, the technical discourse meets the philosophy of science. The condition of objectivity of scientific knowledge is called into question by the Harawayan notion of “situated knowledge”: the subject who thinks is not an autonomous and abstract subject, he always thinks with some intention and from somewhere. Science, Haraway tells us, is not neutral.
Haraway also displaces the debate on the concept of technology, which becomes a debate on the concept of nature. There is no such thing as nature on the one hand and technology on the other, but nature itself is an artifact, the product of the use of a variety of techniques. In the same way, technology is not what opposes nature, but what manufactures, produces and defines the idea of ​​nature.
Biotechnologies are the pinnacle of naturalization technologies. Transgenic food, cosmetics, psychopharmaceuticals, cosmetic surgery or fertility techniques well illustrate his motto: "we are all cyborgs"
